Chapter Summary

General rules for given names and surnames, thirty given names, twenty surnames, twenty nouns with a few variants, and enough other stuff that you should only need to look elsewhere for names if you want to.

When naming Muggle-born characters, I recommend that you check the popularity of baby names in the year of that character's birth, like this list (it's set for 1980 right now, but goes all the way back to 1900). Besides just looking at lists, you could also consult Prenomstat. The website is in French, and you can find an English translation here if you want, but that shouldn't be necessary: just type in a name and it'll provide a chart to show that name's popularity by year (with a blue or pink line according to gender).

For Half-bloods, it's a little more complicated, with no hard and fast rules. The child of a Muggle-born might be given a very wizardly name to help them fit in, especially if their surname is a wizarding surname. If a Pure-blood marries a Muggle, then they might have an interest in Muggle culture in general, but they could just as easily be very attached to a traditional family name.

Given Names

These names are mostly drawn from a pool of names common in medieval and/or early 20th century France, with a few oddments that I liked.

  • Names frequently refer to Catholic saints, especially those on the “calendar of saints.” From the list below, these include Jacques, Jehan / Jean, and Pierre, as well as their feminine variants. Saints that are especially important to magical Catholics include St. Albert the Great (Aubert, in French), St. Caspar the Magus (Gaspard), St. Columba of Clonard (m), St. Columba of Spain (f), and St. Cyprian of Antioch (Cyprien).
  • Cog and Star pointed out that the days of the French Republican calendar would be useful as well: Coriandre Bataillier, Mandragore l'Estrange, Lapin Bonmont, etc.
  • Names may be hyphenated, e.g. Jean-Marcel, and hyphenated names may mix masculine and feminine, e.g. Nicole-George. In such cases, the first name is expected to correspond to the person's gender. For men, the feminine component is typically Marie, e.g. Louis-Marie, Estienne-Marie.
  • Men may have feminine names, though they typically have at least one other given name, which is masculine and typically used in day to day life.
  • Middle names do not exist as such. They are not initialed, and given names other than that which the person prefers are simply not used in most situations, e.g. Guillaume Jehan Malfoi might be called Guillaume Malfoi, but never Guillaume J. Malfoi, though his full name might be used on contracts and government documents.

Masculine names may be made diminutive with the addition of -et, e.g. Jacquet; -on, e.g. Thomasson; -ot, e.g. Pierrot; or -ou, e.g. Lou. In Old and Middle French, -eau may be used in place of -ou, e.g. Marceau; and -el and -in may also be used, e.g. Jacquel.

Feminine names may be made diminutive with the addition of -ette, Josette; or -elle, i.e. Estienelle. The feminine variant of some masculine names only have a diminutive form, at least in common use. In Old and Middle French, -on may also be used for feminine diminutives, e.g. Alice → Alison, Marie → Marion; and -ine may also be used, e.g. Georgine.

Yesterday's diminutive may be today's contemporary form, e.g. Marc → Marcel.

I've found very little on genderless or non-binary constructions in French, so you'll need to do some extra digging for that.

  1. Albin (m) or Albine (f), meaning “white, bright,” might be used by French parents who want to name their child after the man that defeated Grindelwald but want to choose a French form of the name. In 1991, most bearers of this name are probably in their forties.
  2. André (m) or Andrée (f), meaning “manly, masculine.”
  3. Corentin (m) or Corentine (f), meaning “tempest.” The name is also attested as Breton.
  4. Dominique (m, f), meaning “belonging to the Lord.”
  5. Estienne (m) or Estienette (f), meaning “crown, wreath,” or “that which surrounds.” The contemporary form is Étienne or Stéphane (m) or Étiennette or Stéphanie (f).
  6. Germain (m) or Germaine (f), meaning “brother.” Kerman (m) is a Basque variant.
  7. Georges (m) or Georgine (f), meaning “farmer, earthworker.” Gorka (m) is a Basque variant.
  8. Gérard (m) or Gerardine (f), meaning “spear-brave,” might be used by French parents who want to name their child after Grindelwald but want to choose a French form of the name. It is probably not a very popular name.
  9. Jaques (m) or Jacqueline (f), or Jacky (md), meaning “supplanter.”
  10. Jehan (m) or Jehanne (f), meaning “God is gracious.” The contemporary form is Jean (m) or Jeanne (f).
  11. Joseph (m) or Josèphe or Joséphine (f), meaning “he will add.”
  12. Louis (m) or Louise (f), meaning “famous in battle.”
  13. Lucien (m) or Lucienne (f), meaning “light.”
  14. Marc or Marcel (m) or Marcelle (f), referring to the god Mars.
  15. Nicolas (m) or Nicole (f), meaning “victory of the people,” might be used by parents who want to name their children after Nicolas Flamel, a French wizard of no small reputation. It was also just plain common in Medieval France.
  16. Pierre (m) or Perrette (f), or Pierrick (md), meaning “stone.”
  17. René (m) or Renée (f), meaning “born again.”
  18. Thomas (m) or Thomasse (f), meaning “twin.”
  19. Vespre (m) or Vespree (f), meaning “evening.”
  20. Yves (m) or Yvette (f), or Yvonne (fd), meaning “yew wood.”

Other masculine names include Baptiste, meaning “baptist”; Benoît, meaning “blessed”; Blaise, meaning “lisping”; Lazare, meaning “God is my helper”; and Roger, meaning “famous spear”.

Other feminine names include Catherine, possibly derived from Hecate (as wixen lore will often have it) or meaning “my consecration of your name,” and also held to mean “pure”; Marie, possibly meaning “sea of bitterness” or “beloved”; Perenelle, possibly meaning “yokel”; Suzanne, meaning “lily” or “lotus”; and Ysabeau, “my God is an oath.”

Surnames

French women typically don't take their spouse's last name for legal purposes, but may use it for day-to-day purposes, e.g. signing contracts as Thomassine de la Terroir, but being addressed as Madame Erbier.

Some names in Magical Britain may be extant in France, including l'Estrange, Evreaux (or Avery), Malfoi, and Rosier. Other canonical French names are Beaufort, Castillon, Dumont, Leroy (or perhaps le Roy), Millefeuille, Perrot, Tremblay, Trouche, and Volant.

Given names (hyphenated or otherwise) may also serve as surnames.

  1. Anouilh, meaning “slow worm.”
  2. Augur, meaning “noble spear,” but reminiscent of the word “augury.”
  3. Badeaux, derived from the Occitan badau and meaning “gullible, stupid,” provided here for all your villainous needs.
  4. Bastarache, meaning “border house,” referring to someone who lives in a house by a boundary.
  5. Bataillier, meaning “to battle,” referring to a professional second in duels; and Champion, used for the same purpose.
  6. Baudelaire, meaning “small dagger.”
  7. Durand, meaning “enduring.” It would be very appropriate for a wix, whose lifespan is greater than a Muggle's, as would Jeune, meaning “young,” or Vieux, meaning “old,” or Lejeune or Levieux.
  8. Erbier, meaning “herb seller,” and also referring to sellers of potions.
  9. Hasteeur, meaning “meat chef.” Perhaps it could be condensed to “Hasteur.” Perhaps an English branch of the family might condense it further, to “Hastur.”
  10. Janvier, meaning “January.” The other months of the year are Février, Mars, Avril, Mai, Juin, Juillet, Août, Septembre, Octobre, Novembre, and Decembre.
  11. Langlois, or l'Anglois, meaning “Englishman.” To mean “Englishwoman,” it would be l'Angloise. To be current with modern French, it would be l'Anglais or l'Anglaise.
  12. Larousse, Leroux, or Rousseau, from roux, meaning “red” or “red-haired.” Feel free to mess around with other colors, e.g. azur (azure), blanc (white), bleu (blue), brun (brown), gris (gray), jaune (yellow), noir (black), vert (green), violet (purple).
  13. Maignen, meaning “cauldron maker.”
  14. Ossier, meaning “bone seller.”
  15. Parcheminier, meaning “parchment seller.”
  16. Salé, meaning “salty,” possibly referring to someone who lives near or works in a salt marsh.
  17. Terreur, meaning “terror,” is most often used in reference to the Terror of Revolutionary France, but it is an actual (albeit rare) surname. Terroir, on the other hand, while easily mistaken to mean “terror,” actually refers means “soil; land, country,” and is often used to refer to the “somewhereness” or local distinctions of a variety of wine (or other food that is highly influenced by local conditions). Terroirists are people who care about terroir. Terroirist terrorists terrorize terroirists.
  18. Toussaint, meaning “all saints.”
  19. Vautour, meaning “vulture.”
  20. Voland, meaning “to fly” or “agile.” For wixes, the implication is obvious.

20 French Nouns

Some of these are Old French, others are younger. I mostly went with whichever form I liked best, since a surname could have formed at any point.

The surnames of the nobility typically refer to the holding of their highest title, e.g. d'Orléans, de Saint Marc; but it is possible to have another surname as well, e.g. Anouilh de Puispeu. One might also use la, le, Du, or de La (note what is and is not capitalized).

Bonus #1 Baguette, meaning “stick,” refers to more than just bread. Especially as baguette magique, it may refer to a magic wand, just as bacchetta magica means the same in Italian (perhaps the practice of wand use made its way to France via Italy). Someone that is surnamed Baguette might be a wand-maker ala Ollivander; de la Baguette also sounds nice for such a purpose.

Bonus #2 Boulanger, meaning “baker,” i.e. somebody who makes baguettes, i.e. wandsmith.
